Key Findings
  • Steep rise after 55: Heart disease rates accelerate sharply in the 55–59 age bracket, peaking for those 80+.
  • Male disparity: Males consistently show higher HD rates across all age groups compared to females.
  • BMI correlation: Average BMI remains elevated regardless of age for HD patients, suggesting weight management is universally important.
  • Target group: Males aged 60–74 represent the highest-risk cohort requiring intensive monitoring.
Dr. Sharma's Action

Launch gender-specific screening campaigns for patients aged 55+ with BMI > 28, including mandatory smoking cessation counselling.

Policy Insights
  • 3× rate difference: Patients reporting "Poor" health have over 3× the HD rate of those reporting "Excellent".
  • Self-reporting works: People accurately self-assess their health risk, validating simple survey-based screening.
  • Priority segment: "Fair" health patients are a large group with high intervention potential.
Policy Recommendation

Implement annual heart disease screening for all adults self-reporting "Fair" or "Poor" general health, and subsidise fitness programs in workplaces.

Summary: What the Data Tells Us

Age + Gender are the strongest demographic predictors. Males over 55 have significantly higher HD rates. Prevention must be age-stratified.

Lifestyle is modifiable and high-impact. Stroke history, difficulty walking, and diabetes are strong comorbidities, while physical activity is protective.

Early self-assessment is reliable. Patients who rate their health "Poor" or "Fair" have dramatically higher HD rates — simple surveys can drive early intervention.
